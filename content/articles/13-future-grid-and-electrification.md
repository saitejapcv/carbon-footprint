# Article 13 — The Future Grid: Why Electrification Is the Real Answer

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 2–5 — explains the long-term pathway

---

## Summary

Electrification — switching the things we use (cars, heaters, stoves, water heaters) from fossil fuels to electricity — combined with a clean electricity grid is the single biggest decarbonization strategy available. Almost every use of fossil fuels in everyday life can be replaced with an electric equivalent that is more efficient, cheaper to run, and zero-emission at the point of use. As the grid itself gets cleaner (and most grids are decarbonizing fast), every previously installed electric device automatically gets cleaner too, with no further action from the owner. The IEA calls electrification "the most important energy trend of the 21st century."

For an individual, the practical implication is that the priority sequence is: (1) get onto a green-electricity tariff today, (2) replace gas appliances with electric equivalents as they fail, (3) replace your car with an EV when you next buy, and (4) consider rooftop solar. Done in this order, an average US household can drop its home-and-transport emissions by 70–90% within 15–20 years without changing lifestyle.

## 1. The electrification logic

A gasoline car is about 20–30% efficient at turning fuel into motion. An electric car is about 80–90% efficient at turning electricity from the grid into motion. Even on a fossil-heavy grid, the EV usually wins on CO2. On a clean grid, the EV is essentially zero-emission.

A gas furnace is about 80–95% efficient at turning gas into heat. A heat pump is about 300–400% efficient because it moves heat rather than creating it (i.e., it produces 3–4 units of heat for every 1 unit of electricity used).

## 3. The grid is getting cleaner

- US grid: ~0.55 kg CO2/kWh (2008) → ~0.371 kg CO2/kWh (2022) → projected ~0.10 kg CO2/kWh by 2035.
- EU grid: ~0.45 kg CO2/kWh (2008) → ~0.25 kg CO2/kWh (2022) → projected ~0.05 kg CO2/kWh by 2035.
- China grid: ~0.75 kg CO2/kWh (2010) → ~0.55 kg CO2/kWh (2022) → projected ~0.30 kg CO2/kWh by 2030.
- India grid: ~0.85 kg CO2/kWh (2010) → ~0.70 kg CO2/kWh (2022) → projected ~0.40 kg CO2/kWh by 2030.

*Source:* Ember Climate — CC BY 4.0 — [ember-energy.org](https://ember-energy.org/)

## 4. Solar and wind are now the cheapest new electricity

In 2024, utility-scale solar and onshore wind are the cheapest source of new bulk electricity in most of the world, often below $30/MWh. This is why the grid is decarbonizing faster than most forecasts predicted just 5 years ago.

*Source:* IEA World Energy Outlook — *(verify license)* — [iea.org/reports/world-energy-outlook-2024](https://www.iea.org/reports/world-energy-outlook-2024)

## 5. The "future-proof" home

A future-proof home for the post-carbon transition looks like:

- All-electric appliances (heat pump, induction, heat-pump water heater, heat-pump dryer).
- A smart thermostat that learns the household's patterns.
- A high-efficiency envelope (insulation, air sealing, good windows).
- Either rooftop solar or a green-electricity tariff.
- An EV (or EV-ready wiring) in the garage.
- Battery storage (optional, but increasingly cost-effective).

This home will keep getting cleaner every year as the grid cleans up, without further action.

## 6. What I can do

1. **Audit your home and vehicle** — which fossil-fueled devices do you have?
2. **Prioritize replacement when they fail** — don't replace a working appliance early, but plan for the swap.
3. **Get onto a green-electricity tariff today** — even before you own an EV.
4. **Quote a heat pump** for your next heating-system replacement.
5. **Talk to neighbors** about community solar or heat-pump group-buy programs.
