# Article 03 — Climate Change 101: Why a Footprint Matters

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~6 minutes (about 2 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** foundational context for all 5 stages

---

## Summary

Climate change is the long-term shift in average weather patterns caused by human activities that add greenhouse gases to the atmosphere. Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas), farming certain ways, and destroying forests all release CO2 and other gases that trap heat. Since the late 1800s, the planet has warmed by about 1.1°C on average — enough to melt glaciers, raise sea levels, and intensify storms. The Paris Agreement aims to hold warming well below 2°C, ideally below 1.5°C; reaching that goal requires cutting global emissions roughly in half by 2030 and to net-zero by mid-century.

A personal carbon footprint matters because total emissions are simply the sum of what individuals, governments, and businesses do. No single person can solve climate change alone, but no solution is possible without billions of changed daily choices. Understanding the science makes it easier to choose actions that actually help.

---

## 1. What is the greenhouse effect?

Sunlight reaches Earth's surface and is partly absorbed and partly reflected back as infrared (heat). Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere — CO2, methane, nitrous oxide, water vapor, and a few industrial gases — absorb some of that outgoing heat and re-radiate it, warming the lower atmosphere. This is a natural process that keeps Earth habitable; without it, average surface temperature would be about −18°C instead of about +15°C.

The problem is that humans have thickened the greenhouse-gas blanket by about 50% since pre-industrial times, by burning fossil fuels and changing land use. The extra warming is what we call climate change.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG1 Ch. 2 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-2](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-2/)

## 2. How much have we warmed the planet?

- About 1.1°C above the pre-industrial average (1850–1900 baseline) as of 2024.
- The last decade was the warmest in the instrumental record.
- Sea level has risen about 20 cm since 1900 and the rate is accelerating.
- Arctic sea-ice extent has shrunk about 40% in summer since 1979.

*Source:* NASA GISS / NOAA — public domain — [climate.nasa.gov](https://climate.nasa.gov/) / [climate.gov](https://www.climate.gov/)

## 3. What happens at 1.5°C vs 2°C vs 3°C?

The Paris Agreement aims to keep warming well below 2°C, ideally below 1.5°C. The differences between these levels are huge:

| Warming level | Coral reefs | Heat waves | Sea-level rise | Arctic ice-free summer |
|---------------|-------------|------------|----------------|------------------------|
| +1.5°C | 70–90% lost | 1-in-10-yr event becomes 1-in-5-yr | ~0.4 m by 2100 | Once per ~century |
| +2.0°C | >99% lost | 1-in-10-yr event becomes 1-in-3-yr | ~0.5 m by 2100 | Once per ~decade |
| +3.0°C | Effectively all lost | Most summers extreme | ~0.7 m by 2100, multi-meter after |

*Source:* IPCC SR1.5 Ch. 3 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3](https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3/)

## 4. What is a "carbon budget"?

The world can emit only a finite amount of additional CO2 before reaching a given warming level. This is called the **remaining carbon budget**.

- For a 50% chance of holding warming to 1.5°C, the remaining budget from 2024 is roughly **250 billion tonnes CO2**.
- At current ~40 Gt CO2/yr emission rates, that budget runs out in roughly **6–7 years**.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG1 Ch. 5 / Ch. 12 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-12](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-12/)

## 5. Why individual footprints matter

Global emissions = population × average per-person emissions. With ~8 billion people and ~4.7 t per person per year, the world emits ~38 Gt CO2e/year. To halve emissions by 2030, the average per-person footprint must roughly halve as well, with much larger cuts from high emitters.

*Source:* UNEP Emissions Gap Report 2023 — public domain

## 6. The bottom line

Climate change is not a future problem — it is happening now, getting worse, and is driven by the cumulative total of what all of us do every day. The good news is that the same arithmetic works in reverse: billions of changed daily choices, combined with systemic change, can stabilize the climate.
