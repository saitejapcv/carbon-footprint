# Article 07 — Home Energy: Heating, Cooling, and the Grid

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 2–4 driver; home upgrades can shift a person 1–2 stages

---

## Summary

Home energy — electricity, natural gas, heating oil, and propane — accounts for about 20% of a typical high-income personal carbon footprint, or roughly 2 to 5 tonnes of CO2e per year. The biggest variable is the energy source: coal-fired electricity produces about 0.9 kg CO2e per kWh; the US national grid average is about 0.371 kg; wind and solar are about 0.011–0.045 kg. Heating fuels also vary widely: natural gas produces about 53 kg CO2e per therm, fuel oil about 10 kg per gallon. The good news is that home energy is the easiest category to decarbonize through technology: heat pumps, induction stoves, rooftop solar, and green-electricity tariffs all exist today and can cut home emissions by 50–90% in a few years.

The two highest-impact changes are (1) switching to a heat pump for heating and cooling and (2) switching to a green-electricity tariff or installing rooftop solar. Combined, these can move a typical US household from Stage 4 down to Stage 2 or 3 in climate terms.

## 1. The numbers behind your utility bill

### 1.1 Electricity

| Grid source | kg CO2e / kWh |
|-------------|---------------|
| US national average (2022) | **0.371** |
| US coal-heavy region | ~0.9 |
| US natural gas-heavy region | ~0.4 |
| US hydro-heavy region (e.g., Pacific NW) | ~0.05 |

| Solar PV (utility scale) | ~0.045 |
| Onshore wind | ~0.011 |

A typical US home uses ~11,000 kWh/year, emitting about **4 t CO2e/yr** from electricity alone on the current grid mix. On a clean grid, that drops to ~0.13 t.

## 2. Heat pumps — the single biggest home upgrade

A heat pump moves heat rather than burning fuel. Modern cold-climate heat pumps work efficiently down to −15°C and use about **3× less energy** than a gas furnace or electric resistance heater. For a typical home, switching from a gas furnace to a heat pump cuts home-energy emissions by about **50–70%**.

*Source:* IEA heat-pump report — [iea.org/reports/heat-pumps](https://www.iea.org/reports/heat-pumps) *(verify license)*

## 4. Daily behavior changes (smaller impact but free)

- Lower heating thermostat 1°C in winter (saves ~6% of heating fuel).
- Raise AC thermostat 1°C in summer.
- Run dishwasher and washing machine only when full.
- Air-dry clothes when possible.
- Turn off lights and electronics when not in use (small impact — better as habit than as primary lever).

*Source:* EPA household guidance — public domain

## 5. The future: your grid is getting cleaner

The US grid has gone from about 0.55 kg CO2/kWh in 2008 to about 0.37 kg CO2/kWh in 2022 — a ~33% drop in 14 years. Solar and wind are now the cheapest source of new electricity in most of the world. If you electrify your home now, your emissions automatically fall over time as the grid cleans up.

*Source:* EPA eGRID — public domain — [epa.gov/egrid](https://www.epa.gov/egrid)

## 6. What I can do this week

1. **Audit your utility bill** — find your annual kWh and therms.
2. **Switch to a green-electricity tariff** if your utility offers one.
3. **Get a heat-pump quote** for heating and cooling when your current system needs replacement.
4. **Lower the thermostat 1°C** tonight.
5. **Replace one fossil-fueled appliance** with an electric one when it fails.
