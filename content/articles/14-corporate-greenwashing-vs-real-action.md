# Article 14 — Corporate Greenwashing vs. Real Action

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~6 minutes (about 2 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 4–5 — how to spot false claims

---

## Summary

As climate concern grows, companies increasingly make climate claims that sound impressive but amount to little. The most common patterns are: vague "net zero by 2050" pledges without interim targets, heavy reliance on cheap carbon offsets instead of actual reductions, "carbon-neutral shipping" or "carbon-neutral flights" claims built on questionable offsets, and misleading carbon-footprint labels on products. UNEP and the IPCC have both called out the gap between corporate climate rhetoric and actual emissions cuts. The Climate Action Tracker finds that no major emitting sector is on track for a 1.5°C-aligned path, even though nearly all major companies have signed climate pledges.

For an individual, learning to spot the difference between real corporate climate action and greenwashing helps you make better consumer choices, vote more effectively, and avoid being misled into thinking a purchase was "carbon-neutral" when it wasn't.

---

## 1. The four warning signs

### 1.1 "Net zero by 2050" with no interim target

A 2050 net-zero pledge is meaningless without a clear 2030 and 2040 milestone and a credible plan to reach it. Watch for: missing interim targets, vague "scopes" (Scope 1, 2, 3), and no mention of absolute reductions versus intensity reductions.

### 1.2 Heavy reliance on cheap offsets

If a company's climate plan leans heavily on forestry offsets or "avoided emissions" credits, it almost certainly is not delivering real reductions. UNEP's 2023 report on offset integrity is the standard reference.

*Source:* UNEP "Integrity Matters for Net-Zero" — public domain — [unep.org](https://www.unep.org/)

### 1.3 "Carbon-neutral" product labels

"Carbon-neutral shipping," "carbon-neutral flight," "carbon-neutral product" labels that rely on cheap offsets are routinely misleading. The offsets typically overstate climate benefit by 2–10×.

### 1.4 Confusion between emissions reductions and emissions avoidance

Some companies claim "avoided emissions" — e.g., "Our product helped customers avoid X tonnes of CO2." Avoided emissions are speculative and cannot be verified the same way direct reductions can.

## 2. What real action looks like

A genuinely climate-committed company will have:

- **Science-based targets** validated by SBTi (Science Based Targets initiative) with absolute Scope 1, 2, and 3 reductions.
- **Interim milestones** (e.g., 50% absolute reduction by 2030 from a 2020 baseline).
- **Real capex** on internal decarbonization (renewable PPAs, fleet electrification, supply-chain engagement).
- **Disclosed lobbying** consistent with its climate targets (no funding of climate-opposing trade groups).
- **Offset use limited to genuinely-unavoidable residual emissions** using high-integrity removal projects.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG3 Ch. 13 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg3/chapter/chapter-13](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg3/chapter/chapter-13/)
*Source:* Climate Action Tracker — *(verify license)* — [climateactiontracker.org](https://climateactiontracker.org/)

## 3. How to spot it as a consumer

| Claim | Likely real? |
|-------|---------------|
| "We reduced our absolute Scope 1+2 emissions by 30% from a 2015 baseline" | Yes, if verified |
| "Our products are 100% carbon neutral via offsets" | Probably greenwashing |
| "Net zero by 2050" without interim targets | Insufficient |
| "Our supply chain is on track for 1.5°C" | Verify with their published SBTi target |
| "We are powered by 100% renewable electricity" | Check for PPAs vs RECs (Renewable Energy Certificates); PPAs are real, RECs may be greenwashing |

## 4. What I can do

1. **Check corporate claims on Climate Action Tracker** before believing them.
2. **Look for SBTi-validated targets** before trusting "net zero" claims.
3. **Avoid "carbon neutral" product add-ons** that rely on cheap offsets.
4. **Vote with your wallet** by supporting companies with verified absolute reductions.
5. **Vote with your vote** by supporting regulation that forces disclosure (e.g., mandatory climate disclosure, ban on misleading green claims).
