# Article 11 — Why Carbon Offsets Usually Don't Work

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~6 minutes (about 2 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 4–5 — important for high emitters considering "net zero" claims

---

## Summary

A carbon offset is a credit representing one tonne of CO2 reduced, avoided, or removed somewhere else, sold to a buyer to "compensate" for their own emissions. Offsets are widely marketed by airlines, fossil-fuel companies, and lifestyle brands as a way to keep emitting while going "carbon neutral." UNEP's landmark 2023 report, "Integrity Matters for Net-Zero," found that many voluntary offset schemes systematically overstate their climate benefit and that the market lacks integrity checks. Key problems include additionality (would the reduction have happened anyway?), permanence (do forests stay forests?), leakage (does protection in one place just shift deforestation elsewhere?), and double-counting.

For an individual trying to reduce their footprint, the simple rule is: **reduce your own emissions first, then offset only the unavoidable remainder using a high-integrity removal project, and treat any "carbon neutral" product claim with skepticism.**

---

## 1. The four big problems with offsets

### 1.1 Additionality

Does the offset represent an emission reduction that would not have happened without the offset money? Many forest-protection projects would have been protected anyway — either by existing law or because the trees were worth more standing than logged. If that's the case, the offset represents no real climate benefit.

### 1.2 Permanence

If you pay to protect a forest that absorbs CO2 today, but the forest burns down in 10 years, the carbon goes back into the atmosphere. Trees are not a permanent carbon store; geological storage or mineralization is.

### 1.3 Leakage

If you protect a forest in country A, but logging activity simply moves to country B, the net benefit is zero. Leakage can be very hard to measure.

### 1.4 Double counting

The same emission reduction can sometimes be counted by both the project developer and the host country toward its Paris Agreement pledge. Voluntary offset schemes have historically struggled to prevent this.

## 2. What high-integrity looks like

A small set of offset types meets a higher bar:

- **Direct air capture (DAC) and storage** — machines that physically remove CO2 from ambient air and store it underground. Expensive (~$200–600/tonne) but genuinely additional and permanent.
- **Mineralization** — reacting CO2 with minerals to form stable carbonates. Permanent but expensive and limited in scale.
- **Methane capture from landfills and coal mines** — short-lived but powerful (methane is 28× CO2).
- **High-quality avoided-deforestation projects** with strong baselines, monitoring, and permanence provisions — rare.

## 3. The IPCC and UNEP guidance

The IPCC AR6 WG3 report (2022) and the UNEP "Integrity Matters" report both warn that most offset schemes do not deliver the climate benefit they claim. The IPCC recommends that offsets be used only for genuinely hard-to-abate residual emissions, not as a substitute for direct reductions.

## 4. What this means for individuals

- An airline selling you a $30 "carbon-neutral flight" add-on is unlikely to represent a real, additional tonne of CO2 avoided.
- A lifestyle brand's "carbon-neutral shipping" claim using cheap offsets may overstate the benefit by a factor of 2–10.
- The most credible path for an individual is: reduce what you can, electrify what you can, then offset only the genuinely-unavoidable remainder with a high-integrity removal project.

## 5. What I can do

1. **Treat "carbon neutral" claims skeptically** — ask which scheme, what type, and what price per tonne.
2. **Prefer direct reduction over offsets** — it is almost always cheaper and more credible.
3. **If you do offset, choose DAC, mineralization, or methane capture** over cheap forestry.
4. **Support policy that tightens offset rules** rather than relying on individual offset purchases.
