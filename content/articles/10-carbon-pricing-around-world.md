# Article 10 — Carbon Pricing Around the World

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 4–5 systemic action; what governments do

---

## Summary

About 75 carbon-pricing schemes are now operating worldwide, covering roughly 25% of global emissions. They come in two main forms: **carbon taxes** (a direct price per tonne of CO2) and **emissions trading systems (ETS)** (a market for emission permits, with the price set by supply and demand). The most ambitious schemes — Sweden ($130/tonne), Switzerland ($100/tonne), and the EU ETS ($80–90/tonne) — have demonstrably cut emissions while growing their economies. The least ambitious — many emerging-economy schemes at under $10/tonne — have negligible impact. The OECD estimates that to stay on a 1.5°C-aligned path, carbon prices need to reach roughly $100–150/tonne across most of the economy by 2030.

For individuals, carbon prices show up in three places: (1) fuel taxes at the pump, (2) electricity bills (when utilities are covered by a scheme), and (3) vehicle-purchase taxes based on CO2 emissions. Carbon pricing is widely considered the most cost-effective climate policy by mainstream economists and the IPCC, because it lets the market find the cheapest emissions cuts across the whole economy.

---

## 1. Two main designs

### 1.1 Carbon tax

A government sets a direct price per tonne of CO2 emitted. Fuel suppliers pay the tax and pass it on. Sweden's CO2 tax is the classic example.

- **Pros:** predictable, transparent, simple to administer.
- **Cons:** politically harder to set the price high enough.

### 1.2 Emissions trading system (ETS)

A government caps total emissions and issues permits. The market sets the price. Examples: EU ETS, California Cap-and-Trade, China national ETS.

- **Pros:** guaranteed emissions outcome (the cap).
- **Cons:** price is volatile; political pressure can weaken the cap.

## 3. What works

Sweden's CO2 tax, in place since 1991 and now ~$130/tonne, has cut emissions by about 27% since introduction while the economy grew ~80%. Switzerland's $100/tonne tax has cut per-capita emissions by ~25% over 15 years. The EU ETS has cut emissions from covered sectors by ~37% since 2005. These are the strongest real-world proof points for carbon pricing as a tool.

*Source:* OECD / EU Commission — public domain / CC BY 4.0

## 4. What doesn't work

- **Very low prices** (under ~$30/tonne) typically do not change behavior.
- **Free permits** to industry (as the EU ETS did initially) undermine effectiveness.
- **Loopholes for heavy industry** can shift production to uncovered sectors ("leakage") — the EU's CBAM is designed to address this.
- **Offsetting emissions** inside an ETS or tax can be gamed; UNEP's "Integrity Matters" report recommends strict limits.

*Source:* UNEP "Integrity Matters for Net-Zero" — public domain — [unep.org](https://www.unep.org/)

## 5. Carbon fines for individuals

There are very few direct individual carbon fines in the world. Most pricing flows through fuel taxes, electricity bills, and vehicle-purchase taxes (France's vehicle malus can reach €60,000 for the highest-emitting new cars). However, businesses face substantial penalties under EU ETS, California Cap-and-Trade, and similar schemes.

## 6. What this means for you

- If you live in a country with a high carbon price, a large share of your "tax burden" is already a carbon price — and it is one of the most effective tools humanity has for cutting emissions.
- If your country has no carbon price, advocating for one is among the highest-leverage climate actions a citizen can take.

## 7. What I can do

1. **Find out the carbon price in your country** on the OECD or World Bank dashboard.
2. **Vote and advocate** for stronger carbon pricing where you live.
3. **Support revenue recycling** — many carbon-tax schemes return the revenue as a per-capita dividend, which protects low-income households.
4. **Avoid paid offset schemes** as a substitute for pricing — most lack integrity.
