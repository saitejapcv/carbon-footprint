# Article 04 — The 5 Stages of a Personal Carbon Footprint

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** explains all 5 stages

---

## Summary

This site uses a five-stage landscape — from a lush green ecosystem (Stage 1) to a barren wasteland (Stage 5) — to make the abstract idea of tonnes of CO2 feel concrete. Each stage corresponds to a range of per-person annual emissions: 0–4 t (Stage 1), 4–8 t (Stage 2), 8–12 t (Stage 3), 12–16 t (Stage 4), and 16+ t (Stage 5). The thresholds are anchored to the IPCC's 1.5°C target of about 2.5 tonnes per person by 2030 and to the current global average of about 4.7 tonnes per person. A typical US or Canadian adult lands in Stage 4; a typical EU or UK adult lands in Stage 3; a typical Indian or African adult lands in Stage 1–2.

The five stages are not moral judgments — they are simply bands on a continuous scale that scientists use to talk about cumulative global risk. Knowing your stage is the first step toward moving down the scale.

---

## 1. The scale, in plain numbers

| Stage | Score band | Tonnes CO2e/yr | What this looks like in real life |
|-------|------------|----------------|------------------------------------|
| 1 — Lush | 0–20% | 0 – 4 | At or below 1.5°C target; ~1 transatlantic flight, ~3 yrs of car driving |
| 2 — Early damage | 21–40% | 4 – 8 | Around global average; typical Indian or Chinese adult |
| 3 — Tipping point | 41–60% | 8 – 12 | EU/UK/Japan average; well above 1.5°C-aligned path |
| 4 — Severe damage | 61–80% | 12 – 16 | US/Canada/Australia average; typical "comfortable" Western lifestyle |
| 5 — Collapse | 81–100% | 16 – 20+ | Top 10% global emitters; large homes, frequent flights, big cars |

*Sources:* UNEP Emissions Gap 2023 (public domain) + IPCC SR1.5 (public domain)

## 2. Stage 1 — Lush, thriving ecosystem (0–4 t)

**On-screen caption:** *With per-person emissions this low, ecosystems stay in balance: forests grow, rivers run clear, and most species can adapt to local conditions.*

What the science says:
- Half of all tree species still show no climate-driven range loss at this emissions level.
- ~70–90% of warm-water coral reefs remain viable.
- River flows and seasonal cycles remain close to historical norms.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG2 Ch. 2 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2/chapter/chapter-2](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2/chapter/chapter-2/)

## 3. Stage 2 — Early damage (4–8 t)

**On-screen caption:** *Damage is visible but local: trees shed leaves out of season, lakes and rivers develop algal blooms, and bird migrations shift by days to weeks.*

What the science says:
- Rivers and lakes show more algal blooms because warmer water holds less oxygen.
- Tree leaf-out and leaf-drop dates have shifted by 1–2 weeks in temperate regions.
- Birds arrive at breeding grounds several days earlier than they did in the 1970s.

*Source:* EPA Climate Indicators (public domain) + NASA Earth Observatory (public domain)

## 4. Stage 3 — Tipping point (8–12 t)

**On-screen caption:** *The climate has crossed regional tipping points: 70–90% of warm-water coral reefs lost, half of all tree species show drought stress, and once-a-decade heat waves now hit every 2–3 years.*

What the science says:
- Coral bleaching events now occur globally every few years.
- Heat waves that used to happen once a decade now hit major cities every 2–3 years.
- Many mountain glaciers are committed to disappearing within decades.

*Source:* IPCC SR1.5 Ch. 3 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3](https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3/)

## 5. Stage 4 — Severe damage (12–16 t)

**On-screen caption:** *Severe damage: wildfire seasons months longer, plankton declines at the base of ocean food webs, and the first human-caused permanent extinctions of mammals and amphibians.*

What the science says:
- US Western wildfire seasons are ~3 months longer than in the 1970s.
- Plankton populations in many ocean regions have declined measurably.
- Several mammal and amphibian species have gone extinct due to climate-driven causes.

*Source:* EPA Climate Indicators + NOAA Climate.gov — public domain

## 6. Stage 5 — Total collapse (16+ t)

**On-screen caption:** *Total collapse: parts of the Amazon flip from rainforest to savanna, multi-meter sea-level rise locks in over centuries, and large regions of farmland can no longer grow traditional crops.*

What the science says:
- The Amazon is at risk of crossing a tipping point into savanna under this emission path.
- Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheet losses become committed to multi-meter sea-level rise over centuries.
- Major breadbaskets (e.g., Mediterranean, US Southwest, parts of India) face recurring crop failures.

*Source:* Lovejoy & Nobre 2018, *Nature Climate Change* (open-access; verify license) + IPCC AR6 WG1 Ch. 9 — public domain

## 7. Why stages matter

Stages are not moral labels. They are risk bands: the higher the stage, the larger the cumulative contribution to global warming and the higher the probability of severe consequences. A person's stage depends on what they actually do, not what they intend.
