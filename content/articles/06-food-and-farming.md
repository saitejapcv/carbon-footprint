# Article 06 — Food and Farming: What You Eat Shapes the Climate

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 2–4 driver; diet swaps can shift a person 1–2 stages

---

## Summary

Food accounts for roughly 20% of a typical high-income personal carbon footprint — about 2 to 4 tonnes of CO2e per year. The single biggest variable is whether a person eats beef, lamb, and cheese (high emissions) or beans, lentils, vegetables, and tofu (low emissions). Beef produces about 60 kg of CO2e per kilogram of meat, while beans produce about 0.9 kg — a 60× difference. Dairy, poultry, pork, and eggs fall in the middle. Poore & Nemecek's landmark 2018 study in *Science* found that even small individual diet shifts — one beef meal per week replaced with plants — meaningfully reduce emissions, and that the world's highest-impact animal products are also the smallest share of global protein and calories.

The good news about food is that it's one of the easiest categories to change without giving up quality of life. Most people can cut their food emissions by 30–50% by swapping a few high-emission items for low-emission equivalents, without going fully vegetarian or vegan.

## 2. Why beef is so high

Beef cattle emit CO2 from land-use change (deforestation for pasture and feed), methane from enteric fermentation (cow burps), manure management, fertilizer for feed crops, and energy for processing and transport. Methane from cow burps alone accounts for ~40% of beef's lifecycle emissions. Methane is a short-lived but powerful greenhouse gas — about 28× the warming of CO2 over 100 years.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG3 Ch. 7 (Agriculture) — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg3/chapter/chapter-7](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg3/chapter/chapter-7/)

## 4. What about "organic" or "local"?

- **Organic** has roughly the same CO2 footprint as conventional for most crops, sometimes slightly higher per unit of food because yields are lower.
- **Local** only matters if the alternative would otherwise be air-freighted; for food that travels by ship or rail, transport is a tiny fraction of the footprint.

The biggest lever is **what** you eat, not how it was grown or how far it traveled.

*Source:* Poore & Nemecek 2018, *Science* — CC BY 4.0

## 5. Food waste

About **30–40% of food produced globally is wasted**. In high-income countries, most waste happens at the consumer end (restaurants, grocery stores, households). Cutting your household food waste in half roughly cuts your food emissions by 15–25%.

*Source:* UNEP Food Waste Index Report 2024 — public domain — [unep.org](https://www.unep.org/)

## 6. What I can do this week

1. **Swap one beef meal per week** for beans, lentils, or tofu. ~250 kg CO2e saved per year.
2. **Cut cheese portions in half** on pizzas, sandwiches, and pasta. ~150 kg CO2e saved per year.
3. **Plan meals** so leftovers get used. ~100 kg CO2e saved per year.
4. **Buy less beef, buy better beef** when you do — grass-fed, local, and used sparingly.
5. **Try "Meatless Monday"** or one fully plant-based day per week.
