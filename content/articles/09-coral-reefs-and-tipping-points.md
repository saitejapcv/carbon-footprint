# Article 09 — Coral Reefs and Tipping Points: Why 1.5°C vs 2°C Matters

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 3 driver; explains why stage thresholds matter

---

## Summary

Coral reefs are one of the clearest examples of why the difference between 1.5°C and 2°C of warming matters. At 1.5°C of warming, roughly 70–90% of warm-water coral reefs are projected to be lost. At 2°C, that figure rises to more than 99% — essentially complete loss. Coral reefs support about 25% of all marine species and provide food and income to roughly half a billion people, so losing them is both an ecological and a human catastrophe. Reefs also illustrate the broader idea of **climate tipping points**: once a system crosses a critical threshold of warming or stress, it can flip abruptly into a new state that is hard or impossible to reverse. Other tipping-point candidates include the Amazon rainforest, the Greenland ice sheet, the Atlantic ocean-conveyor circulation, and the boreal permafrost.

For individual footprints, the message is that "small" differences in global average temperature correspond to large differences in real-world consequences. Cutting your own footprint by a few tonnes per year contributes, multiplied across billions of people, to whether the world stays near 1.5°C or overshoots into 2°C territory.

## 1. What coral reefs do

- Support roughly 25% of all marine species, despite covering less than 1% of the ocean floor.
- Provide food and livelihoods for ~500 million people.
- Buffer coastlines from storm waves, protecting an estimated 200+ million people from flooding.
- Generate ~$30 billion per year in tourism and fisheries value.

*Source:* UNEP / NOAA Coral Reef Watch — public domain — [coralreefwatch.noaa.gov](https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/)

## 2. Why reefs die in warm water

Corals host tiny algae (zooxanthellae) that photosynthesize and feed the coral in exchange for shelter. When water gets too warm (typically 1–2°C above the local summer maximum), corals expel the algae, turn white ("bleaching"), and start to starve. If the heat lasts too long, corals die.

*Source:* NOAA — public domain — [oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/coral_bleach.html](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/coral_bleach.html)

## 3. The 1.5°C vs 2°C coral difference

| Warming level | Reef outcome |
|---------------|--------------|
| +1.0°C | Localized bleaching, mostly recoverable |
| +1.5°C | 70–90% of warm-water reefs lost |
| +2.0°C | >99% lost (essentially complete) |
| +3.0°C | All warm-water reefs lost; cold-water reefs at risk |

*Source:* IPCC SR1.5 Ch. 3 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3](https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3/)

## 4. What are tipping points?

A **tipping point** is a threshold past which a climate system reorganizes into a new state, often abruptly and sometimes irreversibly on human timescales. Examples:

- **Amazon rainforest** → savanna: triggered around 2–5°C of warming or large deforestation, releasing huge CO2 stores.
- **Greenland ice sheet** → committed ice loss: triggered around 1.5–3°C, locking in ~7 m of sea-level rise over centuries.
- **West Antarctic ice sheet** → committed ice loss: triggered around 1.5–3°C, locking in ~3–5 m of sea-level rise.
- **Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)** → slowdown or collapse: triggered around 2–4°C, would disrupt weather patterns globally.
- **Boreal permafrost** → thaw: triggered around 1.5–2°C, releases massive CO2 and methane stores.
- **Coral reefs** → state shift: triggered around 1.5°C, loss of 70–90% as described above.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG1 Ch. 12 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-12](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-12/)
*Source:* Armstrong McKay et al. 2022, *Science* — [doi.org/10.1126/science.abn7950](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abn7950) *(verify license)*

## 5. What individual footprints have to do with tipping points

The world has a limited "carbon budget" left before reaching 1.5°C, 2°C, and triggering each tipping point. At current emission rates (~40 Gt CO2/yr), the 1.5°C budget runs out in roughly 6–7 years. Cutting your personal footprint by 1 tonne per year, multiplied by hundreds of millions of like-minded people, is what makes the difference between staying below 1.5°C and overshooting into 2°C territory — and that single degree of difference is the difference between losing 70% of reefs and losing almost all of them.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG1 Ch. 5 (Carbon and Other Biogeochemical Cycles) — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-5](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-5/)

## 6. What I can do

1. **Reduce your own stage by 1** — every tonne avoided contributes.
2. **Talk about tipping points** — they make the abstract concrete.
3. **Support policies that accelerate clean-energy deployment** — the cheapest way to stay below 1.5°C.
4. **Reduce air travel specifically** — flights are among the highest-emission per-person activities.
