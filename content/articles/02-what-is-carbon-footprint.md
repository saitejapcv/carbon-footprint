# Article 02 — What Is a Carbon Footprint?

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~6 minutes (about 2 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** foundational — read before exploring stages

---

## Summary

A carbon footprint is the total greenhouse-gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by a person, organization, event, or product. The gas that matters most is carbon dioxide (CO2), but other gases — methane from livestock and gas leaks, nitrous oxide from fertilizer, refrigerants from air conditioners — also count and are converted into "CO2-equivalent" (CO2e) units. Almost every part of modern life has a footprint: driving, flying, heating a home, eating food, and buying manufactured goods. Measuring footprints lets us compare choices on a common scale and see which actions cut emissions the most.

Knowing your carbon footprint is the first step toward reducing it. Once you can see which activities contribute the most — typically transport, home energy, and food for most individuals — you can target the biggest sources rather than guessing. The global average is about 4.7 tonnes per person per year; a sustainable, 1.5°C-aligned target is around 2.5 tonnes per person per year by 2030.

---

## 1. The simple definition

A **carbon footprint** is the total amount of greenhouse gases released into the atmosphere as a result of an activity, a person, a company, or a product's lifecycle. It is measured in **kilograms** or **tonnes of carbon-dioxide equivalent (CO2e)** per year.

The "equivalent" part matters: gases like methane and nitrous oxide warm the planet more per tonne than CO2 does, so their emissions are multiplied by a factor (methane × ~28 over 100 years; nitrous oxide × ~265) before being added to the total.

## 2. What makes up a personal carbon footprint?

A typical high-income personal footprint of ~14 t CO2e/year breaks down roughly like this:

- **Transport** — driving, flying, ride-hailing, deliveries (~40%)
- **Home energy** — electricity, natural gas, heating oil (~20%)
- **Food** — especially meat and dairy (~20%)
- **Goods and services** — clothes, electronics, appliances, services (~20%)

For people in lower-income countries, the food share is usually larger and the transport share is usually smaller.

## 3. Direct vs indirect emissions

- **Direct emissions (Scope 1)** come from things you burn yourself: gasoline in your car, natural gas in your furnace, fuel in a plane.
- **Indirect emissions (Scope 2 and 3)** come from things you use that someone else runs: the electricity your utility generates, the food a farmer grows, the clothes a factory sews.

Almost everything in a modern lifestyle involves indirect emissions, which is why footprints are larger than people expect.

## 4. Why is it measured in CO2e, not just CO2?

Because CO2 is not the only greenhouse gas, but it is the most common. To compare footprints fairly, every greenhouse gas is converted into the amount of CO2 that would have the same warming effect over 100 years. This unit is called **CO2-equivalent**, or **CO2e**.

- 1 kg of methane (from cows, leaks, landfills) = 28 kg CO2e
- 1 kg of nitrous oxide (from fertilizer) = 265 kg CO2e
- 1 kg of HFC refrigerant (from old ACs) = 1,000–4,000 kg CO2e

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG1 Ch. 7 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-7](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/chapter/chapter-7/)

## 5. Why does my individual footprint matter?

The atmosphere does not care where emissions come from. If 8 billion people each emit 4.7 tonnes of CO2e per year, the world emits about 38 billion tonnes of CO2e per year. To keep warming below 1.5°C, the average needs to fall to about 2.5 tonnes per person by 2030 and to about 1.4 tonnes by 2050.

*Source:* IPCC SR1.5 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/sr15](https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/)
*Source:* UNEP Emissions Gap Report 2023 — public domain

## 6. What I can do today

1. **Measure** — use the calculator on this site to see your current footprint.
2. **Find your biggest category** — transport, energy, food, or goods.
3. **Pick one big action and one small action** — replace one car trip per day and one beef meal per week, for example.
4. **Re-measure in 3 months** to see your progress.
