# Article 08 — Climate Inequality: Whose Emissions, Whose Harms

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~7 minutes (about 3 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 4–5 — context for the heaviest emitters

---

## Summary

Climate change is a deeply unequal phenomenon. The richest 10% of the world's population is responsible for about 50% of global emissions, while the poorest 50% are responsible for only about 10%. At the extreme, the top 1% of emitters — roughly the world's multi-millionaires — average about 40 to 50 tonnes of CO2e per person per year, more than 10× the global average and 20× a sustainable target. Meanwhile, the poorest half of humanity bears roughly 75% of the climate damages despite contributing the least emissions. This is not just a moral question: it explains why climate solutions need to focus on the highest emitters and the highest-emitting countries, where each tonne of avoided CO2 buys the most avoided damage.

Climate inequality also runs along country lines: the average US, Canadian, or Australian adult emits roughly 14–15 tonnes per year, while the average Indian or sub-Saharan African adult emits roughly 1.5–2 tonnes per year. Understanding this distribution is essential to designing fair climate solutions that don't punish the world's poorest for the emissions of its richest.

## 3. Why the top 1% emits so much

The richest 1% of the world's population typically:

- Own multiple homes (each with energy use)
- Fly frequently for leisure — sometimes weekly
- Own multiple cars, often large and gasoline-powered
- Consume meat and dairy at high rates
- Invest in fossil-fuel-exposed portfolios, which indirectly fund emissions

The carbon footprint of a single private-jet flight can exceed the lifetime emissions of an average global citizen.

*Source:* World Inequality Lab — CC BY 4.0

## 4. Who suffers most from climate change

- The poorest 50% of the world's population suffer roughly 75% of climate damages.
- Low-lying island nations face existential threat from sea-level rise, despite near-zero emissions.
- Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia suffer the worst agricultural impacts despite emitting the least.
- Marginalized communities within rich countries (low-income, Indigenous, Black, Latino) face disproportionate exposure to heat waves, floods, and air pollution.

*Source:* IPCC AR6 WG2 Ch. 16 — public domain — [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2/chapter/chapter-16](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2/chapter/chapter-16/)

## 5. Why "per capita" is the right framing

Comparing countries or individuals by **per-person** emissions (not total) is essential because the climate problem is the sum of human activities. Per-capita framing puts a US adult (~14 t) and a Malian adult (~0.5 t) on the same scale, and shows that fair-share reductions require high emitters to cut more, faster.

## 6. What this means for solutions

- Climate policy that prices carbon falls hardest on the heaviest emitters — that's the point.
- High emitters have the largest individual effect on global emissions.
- Climate finance from rich countries to vulnerable countries is an obligation, not charity.
- Within countries, low-income households need transition support (heat pumps, EVs, retrofits) so they are not penalized.

## 7. What I can do

1. **Recognize your stage** — and your global rank as an emitter.
2. **Support policies that target the top 10%** — carbon pricing, frequent-flyer levies, mansion taxes, fossil-fuel subsidy reform.
3. **Vote and advocate** for international climate finance.
4. **Talk about climate inequality openly** — it's not about shaming individuals, it's about aligning each person with their fair share.
