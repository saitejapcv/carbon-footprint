# Article 15 — How to Talk to Friends and Family About Climate Change

**Audience:** general public  
**Reading time:** ~6 minutes (about 2 pages)  
**Stage relevance:** Stage 1–5 — social multiplier

---

## Summary

Climate change is hard to talk about because it touches identity, values, money, and fear. Most people respond best to conversations that focus on shared values, concrete actions, and benefits they actually care about — like saving money, cleaner air, healthier food, and a safer future for their kids — rather than guilt, doom, or moral lectures. Research on climate communication finds that messages emphasizing co-benefits (saving money, health, convenience) and showing that others in their community are acting tend to move behavior more than warnings alone. Effective conversations also meet people where they are rather than starting from a presumption of shared concern.

This article offers practical communication patterns drawn from public climate-communication research and from the lived experience of climate organizers. It is not about convincing anyone of anything — it is about being a trusted, non-judgmental source of practical help when people you care about decide they want to act.

---

## 1. Lead with values, not facts

Most people's climate position is downstream of their values and identity, not upstream of new information. Starting with "here's a study" usually triggers defense, not curiosity. Starting with "I care about [our kids / the local river / saving money on utilities]" gives people a shared value to connect to.

## 2. Concrete actions, not abstract targets

People act on concrete, near-term, low-cost actions ("swap one beef meal per week for beans") more than on abstract targets ("reduce your Stage 4 footprint to Stage 2"). Frame suggestions in terms of small, doable steps with co-benefits.

## 3. Talk about co-benefits, not just emissions

Frame every action by what it gives the person, not just what it gives the climate:

| Action | Co-benefit |
|--------|------------|
| Walk/bike to work | Saves gas money, exercise, no commute stress |
| Heat pump | Lower utility bills, more even heating, no combustion in home |
| Plant-based meals | Often cheaper, often healthier, often tastier once you learn |
| Insulation | Lower bills, quieter home, fewer drafts |
| Green-electricity tariff | Often same price, supports renewables |
| EV | Lower fuel and maintenance costs, smoother ride |

## 4. Show that others are acting

People are more likely to act when they see that others in their reference group are acting. Share examples from your community, not just from celebrities or activists.

## 5. Don't shame people for past behavior

Most high-emitters are not choosing to emit — they are living in systems designed around fossil fuels. Shaming them is counterproductive and unkind. Help them with the next step, not the previous one.

## 6. Don't try to convince everyone

Some people are not ready, and that's fine. Focus your energy on people who are open. A 5-minute conversation that helps someone swap one car trip per day is worth more than an argument that convinces no one.

## 7. Be a patient, non-judgmental resource

If you are the friend who knows about heat pumps, expect a few of your friends to ask you about heat pumps. Be the person they ask.

## 8. Practical conversation starters

- "I just learned that switching to a heat pump would save me about $1,000 a year. Want me to send you the link?"
- "We're trying one plant-based day a week — it's actually been fun. Want to try it together?"
- "I cut my flying to once a year and it saved me money. How about you?"
- "Have you seen those new green-electricity tariffs? They're often the same price as regular."
- "I was reading about how much coral reefs matter. Did you know 70% of them could be saved at 1.5°C?"

## 9. The social multiplier

One conversation, if it leads to one action, multiplies through the network. Be the spark for one friend, one neighbor, one family member. That's how Stage 4 average emitters become Stage 2 ones, household by household.
